## premortem: the project failed. why?

imagine it's 6 months from now. we implemented the vision. it failed miserably. what happened?

---

### failure mode 1: squarespace ui redesign mid-batch

**what happened**: squarespace deployed a major ui redesign while the 3-hour batch ran. selectors broke on domain 150. the batch halted. 150 domains were unlocked, 150 remained locked. transfer codes were requested for 150 domains but emails hadn't arrived yet.

**what we missed**: we assumed the ui would be stable for the entire batch run.

**what assumption was wrong**: "squarespace ui selectors remain stable" — true only short-term.

**what risk did we ignore**: ui changes while batch executes.

**mitigation**:
- add selector version check at batch start
- checkpoint progress so batch can resume
- run batches in low-change periods (not squarespace deploy days)

---

### failure mode 2: squarespace rate limited/banned the account

**what happened**: after 100 domains, squarespace detected automated behavior. they temporarily locked the account. we couldn't log in for 24 hours. some transfer codes expired before we could resume.

**what we missed**: we assumed sequential with delays would be enough to avoid detection.

**what assumption was wrong**: "no squarespace rate limits triggered in batch operations"

**what risk did we ignore**: aggressive bot detection that looks at patterns, not just rate.

**mitigation**:
- add randomized delays between operations
- add browser fingerprint randomization
- consider human-in-the-loop for critical operations

---

### failure mode 3: email delivery failed for transfer codes

**what happened**: 300 transfer codes were requested. 50 emails never arrived (spam filter, delivery issues). we had no way to request them again without prior codes becoming invalid. domains stuck in "code requested but never received" state.

**what we missed**: email delivery is unreliable.

**what assumption was wrong**: "transfer codes via email is acceptable" — acceptable but fragile.

**what risk did we ignore**: email deliverability issues at scale.

**mitigation**:
- document email troubleshoot steps
- add email receipt verification step
- batch domains in smaller groups to catch email issues early

---

### failure mode 4: 60-day lock periods for critical domains

**what happened**: 50 of the 300 domains were recently transferred to squarespace or had contact changes. they were in 60-day lock periods. the batch tried to unlock them, failed silently, and continued. user thought all domains were unlocked. transfer at new registrar failed for 50 domains.

**what we missed**: the `lockReason` field wasn't surfaced clearly in the plan.

**what assumption was wrong**: "all domains are transferable" — false for ~15% of domains.

**what risk did we ignore**: silent failures for locked domains.

**mitigation**:
- plan output must highlight locked domains prominently
- add pre-flight check for untransferable domains
- fail-fast on locked domains instead of silent continue

---

### failure mode 5: session timeout in long batch

**what happened**: the 3-hour batch was at domain 280 when the squarespace session expired. the tool tried to continue but operations failed with auth errors. 280 domains were in various states, 20 were never touched.

**what we missed**: session lifecycle in multi-hour operations.

**what assumption was wrong**: "sessions don't timeout in multi-hour batch"

**what risk did we ignore**: squarespace session expiration policy.

**mitigation**:
- add session health checks before each operation
- add auto-re-login on session expiration
- checkpoint progress for resumable batches

---

### failure mode 6: squarespace deployed captcha on domain pages

**what happened**: after domain 50, squarespace started to display captcha on every domain settings page. the batch halted completely. we couldn't automate past the captcha. 50 domains unlocked, 250 locked. no path forward without captcha solver service.

**what we missed**: captcha is the ultimate automation killer.

**what assumption was wrong**: we assumed squarespace wouldn't weaponize captcha against us.

**what risk did we ignore**: existential risk of captcha deployment.

**mitigation**:
- none fully automatic. options:
  - integrate captcha solver service (expensive, ethical concerns)
  - human-in-the-loop for captcha (defeats full automation)
  - abandon automation, do manual
- this is the existential risk. if it happens, the vision fails.

---

### failure mode 7: squarespace banned account for TOS violation

**what happened**: after 100 domains, squarespace's fraud team detected automation patterns. they permanently banned the account for TOS violation. all 300 domains were frozen. we couldn't log in, couldn't transfer, couldn't access any resource. had to contact squarespace support and negotiate.

**what we missed**: we assumed "worst case is account lockout" but "domain freeze" is worse.

**what assumption was wrong**: we assumed lockout means we can try again. frozen domains is worse.

**what risk did we ignore**: squarespace could hold domains hostage.

**mitigation**:
- transfer most critical domains first
- keep human intervention available
- document escalation path with squarespace support
- have legal ready if needed

---

## conclusion

the premortem exposed 7 failure modes:

**mitigable (should address in implementation):**
1. **ui redesign mid-batch** — add resume capability
2. **account rate limit/ban** — add randomized delays
3. **email delivery failures** — batch smaller, verify receipts
4. **60-day lock periods** — surface locked domains prominently in plan
5. **session timeout** — add session health checks and auto-re-login

**existential (no full mitigation, accept the risk):**
6. **captcha deployment** — vision fails if this happens. no automatic mitigation.
7. **TOS ban with domain freeze** — transfer critical domains first. have legal ready.

the existential risks (#6, #7) are why this vision is uncomfortable. we proceed because:
- no evidence of captcha currently
- domains are ours, we're entitled to transfer them
- manual alternative takes 50 hours and has its own risks

**action taken:**
- updated 1.vision.md anti-patterns table with mitigations for #1-5
- added: resumable batches, randomized delays, email verification, locked domain highlight, session health checks, critical-first transfer

all 7 are now documented in vision. #1-5 have mitigations. #6-7 are accepted risks with no full mitigation.
