## the devil's advocate questions

### 1. why might this direction be wrong?

**criticism**: ui automation is fundamentally fragile. squarespace could redesign their ui at any moment, all selectors could break.

**response**: this is a real risk. however, the selectors use data-test attributes with semantic fallbacks (e.g., `[data-test="domain-name"], h1.domain-name`). the maintenance burden is acknowledged in the vision under "cons" and "uncomfortable tradeoffs". the alternative — no api — means we either automate the ui or don't solve the problem.

**verdict**: the risk is real but acceptable given the constraints.

### 2. what would someone who disagrees say?

**criticism #1**: "just do it manually — it's only 50 hours over a few weeks"

**response**: 50 hours of tedious, error-prone manual work vs 3 hours of automated batch. engineer time is expensive. the risk of human error on domain 247 is real (mis-clicks, skipped steps, fatigue). the tool also enables re-verification via re-plan.

**criticism #2**: "wait for squarespace to release an api"

**response**: squarespace has had years to release a domain management api. there is no indication they will. we have 300 domains to move now, not in some hypothetical future.

**criticism #3**: "this solves a one-time problem — will it be used again?"

**response**: partially true. however, the pattern (declastruct + playwright for ui-only platforms) is reusable. other ui-only platforms (legacy cms systems, other registrars) could use the same approach. and even if it's one-time, 50 hours saved is worth the investment.

**verdict**: the disagreements are heard, but the responses are reasonable.

### 2.5. harder arguments against this vision

**criticism #4**: "the complexity is unjustified — declastruct + domain-objects + daos + cache for a one-time migration?"

**response**: this is the strongest argument against the vision. the machinery is substantial:
- domain-objects for type safety
- dao pattern for get/set abstraction
- cache layer for performance
- declastruct integration for plan/apply

a simple procedural loop (`for domain of domains: unlock(); requestCode()`) would work. the extra layers exist because:
1. wish explicitly asked for declastruct pattern consistency
2. idempotency requires state awareness (findsert vs create)
3. 300 domains means errors are likely — recovery needs clear state

still, this is a valid criticism. the vision could be simpler. we chose complexity for consistency and safety.

**criticism #5**: "what's the build vs save ROI?"

**response**: if build took 100 hours to save 50 hours, ROI is negative. estimate:
- playwright scrapers: ~8-12 hours
- domain objects + daos: ~4-6 hours
- declastruct integration: ~4-6 hours
- total: ~16-24 hours to save 50 hours

ROI is positive, but not overwhelming. the margin is thin.

**criticism #6**: "is this against squarespace terms of service?"

**response**: likely yes. squarespace TOS typically forbid automated access. risks:
- account suspension
- domains frozen mid-transfer
- legal action (unlikely but possible)

we accept this risk because:
- the domains are ours
- we're leaving anyway
- the worst case (account ban) just accelerates our departure

**verdict**: these are uncomfortable truths. the vision proceeds despite them.

### 3. what blind spots exist because we want this to work?

**blind spot #1**: we assume squarespace won't detect and block automation.

**response**: sequential process with bottleneck rate limiter mimics human behavior. no evidence of captcha or bot detection in tests. added to "must validate" list.

**blind spot #2**: we assume email delivery is reliable for transfer codes.

**response**: email delivery is outside our control. the vision acknowledges this as "awkward" — the fully-automated dream is slightly broken. user must act on emails promptly.

**blind spot #3**: we assume all 300 domains are transferable.

**response**: some domains may be in 60-day lock periods, grace periods, or disputed states. the `lockReason` field handles this via untransferability indication. added to "must validate" list.

**verdict**: blind spots have been identified and addressed in the vision.

### 4. what would a skeptic point out?

**skeptic**: "ui selectors are brittle. you'll spend more time in maintenance than it saves."

**response**: the selectors use fallback patterns and are centralized in `src/access/sdks/squarespace.via.playwright/selectors/`. maintenance is localized. for a one-time migration of 300 domains, even if selectors break once and take 2 hours to fix, we're still ahead of 50 hours manual work.

**skeptic**: "chromium overhead for what could be http requests"

**response**: there is no api. http requests are not possible. chromium is the only option.

**skeptic**: "no rollback — if a domain gets stuck mid-batch, you're stuck"

**response**: idempotent operations (findsert/upsert) mean you can safely re-run. a "stuck" domain just means re-plan and re-apply. cache invalidation on mutation ensures fresh state. no rollback needed when operations are idempotent.

**skeptic**: "what if squarespace adds captcha to all pages?"

**response**: this is the existential risk. if squarespace weaponizes captcha against automation, the vision fails with no fallback. we accept this risk because:
- no evidence of captcha currently
- the alternative (manual 50 hours) is worse
- squarespace has 300k+ customers who use this UI; a captcha wall would break their UX too

**verdict**: the skeptic's points are valid but addressed.

## conclusion

the devil's advocate exercise exposed weak points at multiple levels:

**surface-level (addressed):**
1. **ui fragility** — mitigated by selector fallbacks
2. **email dependency** — acknowledged as awkward, no alternative
3. **blind spots** — added to validation list

**deeper concerns (accepted without full mitigation):**
4. **complexity unjustified** — valid criticism. we chose consistency over simplicity.
5. **thin ROI margin** — 16-24 hours build to save 50 hours. positive but not comfortable.
6. **TOS violation** — likely against squarespace rules. we're leaving anyway.
7. **existential captcha risk** — no mitigation possible.

**the honest verdict**: this vision has uncomfortable tradeoffs. we proceed because:
- the wish explicitly requested declastruct pattern
- manual alternative is worse (50 hours, error-prone)
- the domains are ours and we want out

the vision holds, but it's not without legitimate criticism.
